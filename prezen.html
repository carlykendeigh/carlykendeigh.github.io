Chapter 2 and 4

#Approximate Analogy

If you could really break it down, songs are very similar to presentations. Obviously presentations have multiple senses engaged (sight, sound, and -- at times -- audience participation), whereas music is typically only heard, but they share many key ideas in their creation and success. Chapter 2 discusses "Preparing, designing, and delivering a presentation is a creative act.", and songwriting is also a creative process. To be a breakthrough, hit single you need innovation in your music as to not bore your audience, similar to the open-mindedness necessary for successful presentations. But, most presentations and songs utilize storytelling and "stickiness"/ catchiness to help the audience better remember their content. Presentations follow an introduction of the topic (many times, an anecdote), an introduction to a problem, the proposed solution or research, and a conclusion. A song has a verse, chorus, second verse, repeated chorus, sometimes a bridge, and an outro. The structures might not be perfectly the same but have enough overlap to see the similarities. Successful presentations are credible, unexpected, and simple; in the music industry, think of Noah Kahan or Taylor Swift. Taylor Swift is now a global sensation, but she began as a creative songwriter who Tim McGraw gave credibility to -- which helped her get her feet under herself as an artist. She crossed genres and completely reinvented herself time and time again to grow in her music as she had in her life. Her most popular songs? Ones with catchy choruses and storytelling verses (Love Story, 22, Anti-Hero, Lover, etc.). Noah Kahan is a newer musical icon. Now being endorsed by artists like Hozier, Lizzy McAlpine, and Zach Bryan, his unique, folk style is renowned for the storytelling feel and emotional lyrics. His songs were unexpected compared to similar artists in the field, and that is part of the reason he is so successful. Artists craft music the way that CEOs, startups, or TEDxTalks speakers create presentations -- relying on creativity, points that stick, storytelling, and credibility.
