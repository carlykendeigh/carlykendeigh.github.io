#Word Journal

Chapters 6 and Colin Breck: Understanding Our Core Values

Authenticity Transparency Colin Breck writes to center a successful company around values, which brings colleagues together to focus on one, authentic, focus. He questioned the stereotypical portrayal of a leader, often involving confidence and boldness. Instead, he embraced a broader definition, one that connects with personal values and accepts vulnerability. He states that embodying values, not just talking about them, is what defines a true leader. After taking the time to identify his own values, Breck could be a more authentic leader and motivate others to do the same. Sharing his values with his team brought them closer and made a more cohesive work environment. Maybe the focus of values is less on authenticity, while it is still very important, but more on transparency — and sharing your needs and strengths with the people you surround yourself with. I took the time after reading to identify some of my key values and what aligns closest to my needs and work ethic: Empathy, Communication, and Creativity. I always look at situations from multiple perspectives, and feel the most successful when I am constantly sharing motivation and communication with my teammates. Creativity is an essential part of my values, without it I get burnt out easily and struggle to find a good reason to keep working, so I adjust to give myself a creative outlet when necessary to stay my most productive self.

#Approximate Analogy

Chapter 7

The hierarchy of a webpage is very similar to labels of shelves and areas in a library. Imagine stepping into a library, greeted by shelves neatly stacked with books. Now, envision these shelves as the visual hierarchy of a website's homepage. The books placed at eye level in the library are akin to the primary content and key actions presented prominently on the website's landing page. Just as these eye-level books are the most accessible and frequently sought, the critical content and primary user actions should be noticeable and easily accessible on the webpage. On the other hand, books on higher or lower shelves in the library represent content of secondary importance, mirroring the placing of less crucial information in a well-structured hierarchy on the website. Much like a library's organized layout aids readers, a website's well-designed homepage helps users find what they need, providing a seamless experience without overwhelming users.

#FreeForm

Pg 137-141

Efficiency is relevant to usability tests, focus groups, and do-it-yourself testing. In a focus group, you can gain insight to people’s reactions to new concepts and gauge their opinions on topics pertaining to your product or site. However, drawing out the process of discussion in focus groups is time consuming — so balancing the use of specific questions and follow up questions allow the participants to get straight to the point while still feeling like their voice is heard. In a usability test, Krug argues the most efficient manner of usability test is do-it-yourself testing. In this method, the expensive and complicated form of user testing is quickened for maximum feedback and repetition opportunities. Using smaller groups to test with and giving less tasks to the groups can shorten the time span. This method is used more frequently in the app development process than the traditional style of user testing, which allows for more modifications to the site based on user feedback before it is pushed out as a final product.

#Tutorial

Pg 137-141 (cont)


Krug describes how various checks of accessibility should be performed on a site, since many accessibility checkers are equivalent to “spell checkers”, they may catch the misuse or lack of alt text, but Krug describes a baseline before going further in depth for accessability testing. I have added the steps one can take to prove these actions will make a positive difference in the site’s functionality.

Add appropriate alt text to every image. Add an empty (or “null”) alt attribute (alt="") for images that screen readers should ignore, and add helpful, descriptive text for the rest. To learn how to write good alt text—and in fact to learn how to do any of the things in this list—head over to webaim.org. The folks at WebAIM have written excellent practical articles covering the nuts-and-bolts details of almost every accessibility technique. Once all have been added, go into the hosted site and inspect all images to verify the alt text is clearly available. Then check the site with images turned off in the accessability settings. If the site still functions as expected, congrats! You can move on to the next step.
Use headings correctly. The standard HTML heading elements convey useful information about the logical organization of your content to people using screen readers and make it easier for them to navigate via the keyboard. Use h1 for the page title or main content heading, h2 for the major section headings, h3 for subheadings, and so on, and then use CSS to redefine the visual appearance of each level. If you were to make a list of what information was most to least important, this can verify that the most important information is coordinated with the organizational structure being implemented with heading tags. If you showed the site to someone who had no idea how to use/ read the website, could they tell you the most important information just from the headings?
Make your forms work with screen readers: This largely boils down to using the HTML label element to associate the fields with their text labels, so people know what they’re supposed to enter. Try testing yourself with a screen reader, make sure that the information to enter is obvious and the input format won't conflict with a system's native auto-filling tendencies.
Put a “Skip to Main Content” link at the beginning of each page. Imagine having to spend 20 seconds (or a minute, or two) listening to the global navigation at the top of every page before you could look at the content. Test by listening yourself and accessing the skip to main content on each page, do a quick ctrl f and verify that it is present on every page that you can access. This is also a great time to make sure the information is presented in the order you want it to -- similar to the organizational structure from above.
Make all content accessible by keyboard. Remember, not everyone can use a mouse. Hotkeys can be incredibly helpful, as well as tab and the arrow keys to navigate and input information into the site. Practice vocal commands on your website as well.
Create significant contrast between your text and background. Don’t ever use light grey text on a dark grey background, for instance. There are plenty of extensions where you can visualize different types of colorblindness or color-impacting conditions. The colorblindness verifications are sometimes lacking, but going through each availability yourself double checks the accessability on your website.
