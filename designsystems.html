#Direct Analysis

LinkedIn

I wanted to compare the findings from the design systems reading to a website I check daily: LinkedIn. The website demonstrates an effort by the design team to achieve a cohesive and efficient layout for their product. However, in my research I noticed some inconsistencies, prompting a targeted approach to design consistency and effectiveness. There are deconstructed large design elements into smaller, atomic components. For instance, they broke down more complex interface elements like user profiles, job postings, and connection requests into fundamental building blocks. These components (profile cards, job tiles, and connection buttons) were organized into a comprehensive style guide and component library. This ensured uniformity in typography, including typefaces (e.g., Arial, Helvetica), font sizes (e.g., 16px, 18px), font weights (e.g., regular, bold), and line-heights across the platform. (All found in LinkedIn’s brand design and style guide manual) The design system emphasized responsive layouts, showcasing how elements like profile pictures and text boxes adapt seamlessly to various screen sizes, from desktops to smartphones. This adaptability ensures a consistent user experience, regardless of the device being used, since the website has desktop and mobile versions. The inclusion of media queries and flexible grids illustrated this responsive design approach. LinkedIn’s evolving design system is exemplified by ongoing efforts to enhance accessibility. For instance, they integrated high color contrast for improved readability, making the platform more inclusive. Additionally, user feedback mechanisms were demonstrated, showcasing how LinkedIn incorporates user suggestions to improve the design system, validating its user-centric values. Incorporating the use of consistent spacing and alignment, the website demonstrated a meticulous attention to detail in spatial systems and grids. Elements such as margins, paddings, and grid layouts were strategically implemented, providing a visually organized and aesthetically pleasing design. Overall, LinkedIn's website layout exemplifies a carefully crafted design system that prioritizes consistency, accessibility, and user-centricity, embodying the brand's commitment to providing an optimal user experience.

#Tutorial

OpenTable

  1. Understanding a Flexible Design Team Structure Consider structuring your design team into product design, user research, marketing design, and brand design. Tailor this structure to fit your organization's unique needs and projects, ensuring teams work collaboratively for effective outcomes.
  2. Embrace Agile Principles in Design Adopt an agile design approach, fostering collaboration, iteration, and cross-functional teamwork. Create project teams comprising designers, engineers, and product managers to iterate, gather insights, and design collaboratively, enhancing adaptability and efficiency.
  3. Cultivate a Collaborative Design Culture Cultivate a culture of collaboration, openness, and inclusivity in your design process. Encourage regular sharing sessions, brainstorming, and involving diverse perspectives from various teams to nurture a collaborative environment, leading to better product outcomes.
  4. Flexible Design Process: Idea to Launch Adopt a flexible design process like the triple-diamond model, emphasizing learning, defining, and refining. Tailor each phase to suit your project's complexity, ensuring alignment with stakeholders and effective problem-solving.
  5. Integrating Usability Testing and Research Integrate both qualitative and quantitative research methods into your design process. Conduct usability testing, user research, A/B testing, and usage analysis to gather insights, validate design decisions, and enhance the overall user experience.
  6. Streamlining Design Handoffs Develop efficient design handoff processes, including clear documentation and well-organized storage systems. Ensure seamless collaboration between teams and effective sharing of design assets to expedite the development process.
  7. Tailored Wireframing Process Customize your wireframing process based on project requirements. Start with low-fidelity wireframes and emphasize ideation and collaboration before moving on to high-fidelity designs. Iterate and refine to achieve optimal results.
  8. Prioritizing Features Effectively Prioritize features by involving cross-functional teams, weighing input from product managers, designers, and developers. Align feature requests with organizational goals and user needs to determine the most impactful and feasible initiatives.
  9. Leveraging InVision for Design Workflow Integrate collaboration tools like InVision for testing, iteration, and stakeholder communication. Utilize it as a platform for rapid prototyping, gathering feedback, and refining designs based on real-world insights.
  10. Best Practices for Crafting Specific User Experiences Implement best practices for reimagining specific user experiences. Focus on deep problem understanding through research, iterative design, and active user involvement. Stay open to unexpected insights, fostering a user-centric approach.
  11. Designing for Diverse User Groups Design with empathy, especially for users less familiar with technology. Prioritize intuitive interfaces, clear communication, and extensive usability testing to ensure a seamless and enjoyable user experience for all users.

Conclusion By adopting a flexible design team structure, agile methodology, collaborative design culture, tailored design processes, usability testing, efficient design handoffs, and leveraging tools like InVision, there can be successful designs and user experiences. Apply these principles, customize them to fit the organization's needs, and draw inspiration from successful models like OpenTable to enhance design workflow and deliver outstanding products.

CNN

CNN's data visualization is a great example of the principles discussed in the article. CNN, being a major news network, does a great job of applying the Visual Information-Seeking Mantra. CNN uses interactive and engaging maps to offer an initial overview of polling results. Users can then zoom in on specific locations, filter based on , and access detailed information about each state. This approach falls in line with the emphasis on providing an overview first and then allowing users to zoom in and filter for more details. CNN's use of interactive charts, graphs, and data-driven visuals in their news articles effectively follows the mantra's principles. These visualizations enable readers to quickly find the main points of a story. If readers wish to learn more, they can interact with the graphics to explore the data in greater detail. CNN is skilled in presenting historical data views. This is especially noticeable in their coverage of the COVID-19 pandemic, where they provide time-series visualizations to depict the progression of cases. This helps viewers understand trends and changes over time, focusing on providing a historical perspective. CNN's user interface is designed to be clear and organized, making sure that viewers can access the information they need without unnecessary complexity. This highlights the efficiency of the website. CNN's use of data visualization effectively embodies these principles through clear overviews, interactive exploration, historical perspectives, and organized visualizations, CNN makes complex data more accessible and understandable for a broader audience.

#Approximate Analogy

Prototyping in Figma

Prototyping can take on many different forms and levels of commitment from designers. To compare to something I have already become familiar with — the prototyping hierarchy is like a stage performance. Musicals or plays take many forms before they debut on stage for an audience, some never make it out of the practicing stage — some go to broadway. Low-fidelity prototypes are the rehearsals, rough sketches where initial ideas are tested and explored, much like the preparatory runthroughs, practices, or workshops for a show. Mid-fidelity prototypes relate to the dress rehearsal, finding the balance between low and high fidelity—similar to the final dress rehearsals where all the elements come together, but with room for adjustments. Finally, high-fidelity prototypes are the grand opening night, rehearsed and refined to closely mirror the final product —performance where everything comes to life. Just as a musical requires teamwork and dedication for success, so does prototyping!

Portfolio

Name: Users need to know the owner of whose work will be presented on the site. Having your name in a visible spot as the link back to the home page lets users clearly know who is the designer.
About: This section is not always found in UI portfolios, but is present more often than not. The About section is a chance for the designer to label their current position and company as well as talk on their experiences and education levels. This gives the designer a deeper personality for the user to relate to.
Work/Resume: This section is the designers space to show off the designs that they have created! They can label what companies they worked with, their teammates, the development process, but every aspect relates back to the individual projects the designer made. This section is the most important, since it shows the designers skills in practice instead of just in descriptions.
Contact: Users want a way to contact designers! How else would designers gain their clients? The option to see more social media platforms or reach out via email are typically seen in portfolios.
Seeing this information about the designs studied, I know that my portfolio needs a more personal touch to showcase my designs. The work can only speak for itself if the site I design for it supports it.
